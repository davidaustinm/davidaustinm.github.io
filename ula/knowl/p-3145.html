  1. The columns of \(AR\) are found by multiplying the columns of \(R\) by \(A\text{.}\) If we remember that a vector multiplied by a matrix forms a linear combination of the columns of \(A\text{,}\) we see that the first column of \(AR\) is \(\vvec_1-3\vvec_2\) and that the other two columns are unchanged.

    Since \(\det R = 1\text{,}\) we have \(\det(AR) = \det A\text{,}\) which says that this operation does not change the determinant.

  2. Multiplying by \(S\) multiplies the determinant by \(3\) since \(\det S=3\text{.}\)

  3. Multiplying by \(P\) changes the sign of the determinant since \(\det P = -1\text{.}\)

  4. If we interchange the first and third columns of \(A\text{,}\) we have

    \begin{equation*} A_1=\left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 \amp -3 \amp 0 \\ 4 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\ \end{array}\right] \end{equation*}

    and \(\det A = -\det A_1\text{.}\) Now we will multiply the first column by \(3\) and add to the second column to obtain

    \begin{equation*} A_2=\left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 4 \amp 13 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\ \end{array}\right] \end{equation*}

    and \(\det A = -\det A_2\text{.}\) Let's interchange the second and third columns so that

    \begin{equation*} A_3=\left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 4 \amp 1 \amp 13 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\ \end{array}\right] \end{equation*}

    so that \(\det A = \det A_3\) and then multiply the second column by \(-13\) and add to the third column so that

    \begin{equation*} A_4=\left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 4 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp -12 \\ \end{array}\right]\text{.} \end{equation*}

    This gives \(\det A = \det A_4 = -12\text{.}\)
