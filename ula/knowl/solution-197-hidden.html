1. We can compute \(A\vvec_1 = 1.4\vvec_1\) and \(A\vvec_2=0.9\vvec_2\text{.}\) This means that \(\vvec_1\) is an eigenvector with associated eigenvalue \(\lambda_1=1.4\) and \(\vvec_2\) is an eigenvector with associated eigenvalue \(\lambda_2=0.9\text{.}\)

  2. Setting up an augmented matrix and row reducing shows that \(\xvec_0=10\vvec_1+14\vvec_2\text{.}\)

  3. We have

    \begin{equation*} \begin{alignedat}{4} \xvec_1\amp{}={}\amp A\xvec_0 \amp {}={} 10(1.4)\vvec_1 \amp {}+{} \amp 14(0.9)\vvec_2 \\ \xvec_2\amp{}={}\amp A\xvec_1 \amp {}={} 10(1.4)^2\vvec_1 \amp {}+{} \amp 14(0.9)^2\vvec_2 \\ \xvec_3\amp{}={}\amp A\xvec_2 \amp {}={} 10(1.4)^3\vvec_1 \amp {}+{} \amp 14(0.9)^3\vvec_2\text{.} \\ \end{alignedat} \end{equation*}

  4. In general, \(\xvec_k = 10(1.4)^k\vvec_1 + 14(0.9)^k\vvec_2\text{.}\)

  5. After a long time, \(k\) becomes large so that \(0.9^k\) becomes very close to zero. This means that \(\xvec_k\approx 10(1.4)^k\vvec_1\text{.}\) So \(P_k\approx10(1.4)^k\) and \(Q_k\approx30(1.4)^k\text{.}\) This means the ratio of \(P_k\) to \(Q_k\) is 1:3.
