  1. Since \(A\) is a positive stochastic matrix, we know that \(\lambda=1\) is an eigenvalue. Therefore, \(A-I\) should not be invertible.

  2. Sage tells us that the reduced row echelon form of \(A-I\) is \(\left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ \end{array}\right]\text{.}\)

  3. The computation tells us that \(A-I\) is invertible. We know that this can't be the case, however, since \(\lambda=1\) is an eigenvalue.

  4. Let's trace through the work that goes into finding the reduced row echelon form of \(A-I\text{:}\)

    \begin{equation*} A-I= \left[\begin{array}{rrr} -0.8 \amp 0.2 \amp 0.4 \\ 0.2 \amp -0.7 \amp 0.1 \\ 0.6 \amp 0.5 \amp -0.5 \\ \end{array}\right] \sim \left[\begin{array}{rrr} -0.8 \amp 0.2 \amp 0.4 \\ 0.0 \amp -0.65 \amp 0.2 \\ 0.0 \amp 0.65 \amp -0.2 \\ \end{array}\right]\text{.} \end{equation*}

    The next step is to multiply the second row by \(0.65/0.65\) and add it to the third row. The last entry in the third row would be \(-0.2 + 0.2(0.65)/0.65\text{,}\) which we identify as being \(0\text{.}\) The problem is that the computer only approximates this result so it finds something close to \(0\) but not quite equal to \(0\text{.}\) It therefore, thinks this is a pivot position and scales the row to have \(1\) in that position.
