1. If \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) and \(c\) are not distinct, we see that \(\det V=0\) because two of the rows are identical. Therefore, we apply the following row operations, assuming \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) and \(c\) are distinct:

    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rl} V = \amp \left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 \amp a \amp a^2 \\ 1 \amp b \amp b^2 \\ 1 \amp c \amp c^2 \\ \end{array}\right] \sim \left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 \amp a \amp a^2 \\ 0 \amp b-a \amp b^2-a^2 \\ 0 \amp c-a \amp c^2-a^2 \\ \end{array}\right] \\ \sim \amp \left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 \amp a \amp a^2 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp b+a \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp c+a \\ \end{array}\right] \sim \left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 \amp a \amp a^2 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp b+a \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp c-b \\ \end{array}\right]\text{.} \end{array} \end{equation*}

    Because of the scalings by \(b-a\) and \(c-a\text{,}\) we have \(\det V = (b-a)(c-a)(c-b)\text{.}\)

  2. If \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) and \(c\) are distinct, then \(\det V \neq 0\text{,}\) which implies that \(V\) is invertible.

  3. In the \(4\times4\) case, we have

    \begin{equation*} \det\left[\begin{array}{rrrr} 1 \amp a \amp a^2 \amp a^3 \\ 1 \amp b \amp b^2 \amp b^3 \\ 1 \amp c \amp c^2 \amp c^3 \\ 1 \amp d \amp d^2 \amp d^3 \\ \end{array}\right] = (d-a)(d-b)(d-c)(c-a)(c-b)(b-a)\text{.} \end{equation*}
