Notice that an eigenvector \(\vvec\) must be in the column space \(\col(A)\) because \(A\vvec=\lambda\vvec\text{.}\) The column space of this matrix is spanned by \(\vvec_1=\threevec{1}{1}{1}\) and we notice that \(A\vvec_1 = 3\vvec_1\text{.}\) Therefore, \(\vvec_1\) is an eigenvector with associated eigenvalue \(\lambda_1=3\text{.}\)

    We also know that that the rank of this matrix is 1 so \(\nul(A)\) is two-dimensional. A basis for the null space is \(\vvec_2=\threevec{-1}{1}{0}\) and \(\vvec_3=\threevec{-1}{0}{1}\) so these vectors are eigenvectors with associated eigenvalue \(\lambda_2=\lambda_3 = 0\text{.}\)

  2. In the same way, \(\vvec_1=\threevec{1}{2}{3}\) is an eigenvector with associated eigenvalue \(\lambda_1=6\) and \(\vvec_2=\threevec{-1}{1}{0}\) and \(\vvec_3=\threevec{-1}{0}{1}\) are eigenvectors with associated eigenvalue \(\lambda_2=\lambda_3=0\text{.}\)
