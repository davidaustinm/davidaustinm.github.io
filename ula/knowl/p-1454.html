  1. Finding the reduced row echelon form

    \begin{equation*} \left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 \amp 2 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\ -2 \amp 0 \amp 2 \end{array}\right] \sim \left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 \amp 0 \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{array}\right] \end{equation*}

    shows that \(\vvec_3\) is a linear combination of \(\vvec_1\) and \(\vvec_2\text{.}\) Therefore, \(\span{\vvec_1,\vvec_2,\vvec_3}\) is the plane defined by \(\vvec_1\) and \(\vvec_2\text{.}\)

  2. We see that

    \begin{equation*} \left[\begin{array}{rrr|r} 1 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp a \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp b \\ -2 \amp 0 \amp 2 \amp c \\ \end{array}\right] \sim \left[\begin{array}{rrr|r} 1 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp a \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp b \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 2a-4b+c \\ \end{array}\right]\text{.} \end{equation*}

    If the equation is consistent, we must have \(2a-4b+c=0\text{.}\) The solution space is the plane defined by \(\vvec_1\) and \(\vvec_2\text{.}\)

  3. In that case, there would be two equations involving the variables \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) and \(c\text{,}\) and the solution space would be a line.
