Let's look in \(\real^2\) and consider \(S\text{,}\) the set of vectors lying on the \(x\) axis; that is, vectors having the form \(\twovec{x}{0}\text{,}\) as shown on the left of FigureÂ 3.5.6. Any scalar multiple of a vector lying on the \(x\) axis also lies on the \(x\) axis. Also, any sum of vectors lying on the \(x\) axis also lies on the \(x\) axis. Therefore, \(S\) is a subspace of \(\real^2\text{.}\) Notice that \(S\) is the span of the vector \(\twovec{1}{0}\text{.}\)
