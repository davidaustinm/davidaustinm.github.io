  1. We have the relationships

    \begin{equation*} \begin{alignedat}{4} J_{k+1} \amp {}={} \amp \amp \amp \amp \amp 0.4 A_k \\ Y_{k+1} \amp {}={} \amp 0.8 J_k \\ A_{k+1} \amp {}={} \amp \amp \amp 0.9Y_k \amp {}+{} \amp 0.8A_k\text{.} \\ \end{alignedat} \end{equation*}

  2. The matrix \(A=\left[\begin{array}{rrr} 0 \amp 0 \amp 0.4 \\ 0.8 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0.9 \amp 0.8 \\ \end{array}\right]\text{.}\)

  3. There is a real eigenvalue \(\lambda_1=1.058\text{,}\) which is larger than \(1\text{.}\) The other eigenvalues are complex \(\lambda_{2,3} = -0.128\pm 0.506i\) and satisfy \(|\lambda_{2,3}| \lt 1\text{.}\) Therefore, the complex eigenvalues will pull vectors in toward the line defined by the eigenvector \(\vvec_1\text{.}\) After a long time, the population is \(\xvec_k\approx c_1(1.058)^k\vvec_1\text{.}\) All the populations grow annually by a factor of \(1.058\) or 5.8%, and there are approximately 38 juveniles and 29 yearlings for every 100 adults.

  4. We now have eigenvalues \(\lambda_1=1.011\) and \(\lambda_{2,3} = -0.106\pm 0.450i\text{.}\) This shows that the growth rate is lowered to \(1.011\) or \(1.1\)% annually.

  5. The first eigenvalue is \(\lambda_1=0.957\) so the growth rate is about \(96\)%. In other words, the population decreases every year.

  6. To be stable, we need the first eigenvalue \(\lambda_1=1\text{.}\) If we experiment with different birth rates, we see that a birth rate of about 0.278 gives this eigenvalue.
