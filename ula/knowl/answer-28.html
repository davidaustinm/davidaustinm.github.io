Answer 2.1.3.1.

  1. The vector \(\bvec\) cannot be expressed as a linear combination of \(\vvec_1\text{,}\) \(\vvec_2\text{,}\) and \(\vvec_3\text{.}\)

  2. We find vectors

    \begin{equation*} \vvec_1=\threevec{3}{1}{-1}, \vvec_2=\threevec{2}{0}{-1}, \vvec_3=\threevec{-1}{2}{3}, \bvec=\threevec{4}{0}{1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

  3. Yes, \(\bvec\) can be expressed as a linear combination of \(\vvec_1\text{,}\) \(\vvec_2\text{,}\) and \(\vvec_3\) in infinitely many ways.

  4. No.

  5. Yes, \(\bvec\) can be expressed as a linear combination of \(\vvec_1\text{,}\) \(\vvec_2\text{,}\) and \(\vvec_3\) in exactly one way.

  6. Any vector \(\bvec\) can be expressed as a linear combination of \(\vvec_1\text{,}\) \(\vvec_2\text{,}\) and \(\vvec_3\) in exactly one way.
