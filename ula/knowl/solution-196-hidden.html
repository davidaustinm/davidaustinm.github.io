  2. The standard basis vectors \(\evec_j\) are eigenvectors associated to the diagonal entries.

  3. Yes. Since there are three distinct eigenvalues, the multiplicity of each eigenvalue must be one. Therefore, the dimension of each eigenspace is one. If we choose a basis vector for each of the eigenspaces, we will obtain a basis for \(\real^3\text{.}\)

The eigenvalues are determined by \(\det(A-\lambda I) = -\lambda^3+4\lambda = -\lambda(2-\lambda)(-2-\lambda)\text{,}\) which shows that the eigenvalues are \(\lambda=0,2,-2\text{.}\) This shows that \(A\) is not invertible since \(\lambda=0\) is an eigenvalue. There is, however, a basis for \(\real^3\) consisting of eigenvectors of \(A\) since the three eigenvalues are distinct.

  5. If \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(A\text{,}\) then \(\lambda^2\) is an eigenvalue of \(A^2\text{.}\) Therefore, the characteristic polynomial of \(A^2\) is \(\det(A^2-\lambda I) = (16-\lambda)(4-\lambda)(1-\lambda)\text{.}\)
