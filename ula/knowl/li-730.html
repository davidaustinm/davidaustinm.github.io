Item 2.2.6.11.c.

We will now explain the relationship between the previous two solution spaces. Suppose that \(\xvec_h\) is a solution to the homogeneous equation; that is \(A\xvec_h=\zerovec\text{.}\) We will also suppose that \(\xvec_p\) is a solution to the equation \(A\xvec = \bvec\text{;}\) that is, \(A\xvec_p=\bvec\text{.}\)

Use the Linearity Principle expressed in PropositionÂ 2.2.3 to explain why \(\xvec_h+\xvec_p\) is a solution to the equation \(A\xvec = \bvec\text{.}\) You may do this by evaluating \(A(\xvec_h+\xvec_p)\text{.}\)

That is, if we find one solution \(\xvec_p\) to an equation \(A\xvec = \bvec\text{,}\) we may add any solution to the homogeneous equation to \(\xvec_p\) and still have a solution to the equation \(A\xvec = \bvec\text{.}\) In other words, the solution space to the equation \(A\xvec = \bvec\) is given by translating the solution space to the homogeneous equation by the vector \(\xvec_p\text{.}\)
