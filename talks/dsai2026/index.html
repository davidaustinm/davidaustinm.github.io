Supporting VI Readers and Authors

Accessible Mathematical Diagrams Created in PreFigure

David Austin Neha Jadhav Alexei Kolesnikov Jason Siefken Volker Sorge
Grand Valley State University Progressive Accessibility Solutions Towson University University of Toronto University of Birmingham

DSAI 2026 – Alexandroupolis, Greece

Motivation

Mathematical diagrams are central to communication in STEM.

  • There is considerable work to support reading diagrams, but little support for authoring diagrams.

  • PreFigure is an authoring system that prioritizes the creation of accessible diagrams by all authors.

  • PreFigure diagrams can be authored inside the PreTeXt authoring system for an accessible workflow.

PreFigure

An author writes a high-level description of the diagram.

  • One description, many outputs:

    • annotated SVG for the web,

    • PDF for print,

    • tactile embossing.

  • Semantic markup: high-level descriptions encourage authors to focus on the ideas they wish to express.

User study

  • Stage 1 of the study, at the Devnar School for the Blind, Hyderabad.
  • 18 Grade 10 students (17 blind, 1 low vision), over three days: baseline, training, evaluation.
  • 15 students participated all three days; the remaining 3 are in a follow-up study.
  • Day 3, a side-by-side observation: tactile media vs the PreFigure Playground with a screen reader.

Observations: tactile materials

  • Warped paper distorted judgments of side length.
  • 2-D / 3-D confusion: an ellipse felt like a sphere.
  • Braille labels misinterpreted, especially when omitted at intersections.
  • Diagram questions are often exam-exempted — so diagrams get under-taught.

Observations: the Playground

  • The keys were learnable: all but one student navigated easily.
  • Self-reported strong comfort with computers; reading diagrams on a computer mostly manageable.
  • One student was notably stronger on digital than tactile.
  • Diagcess audio overlapped with NVDA; focus did not always land on the diagram.

Conclusions, and what’s next

Design lessons for accessible-diagram tools:

  • Predictable conventions, with explicit confirmation of actions and rendering.
  • Clearer communication of measurements, and robust error feedback.
  • Screen-reader coordination: focus management and non-overlapping audio.

Accessibility must support creation, not only consumption. Computer-based exploration complements tactile materials; it does not replace them. Next: refine the Playground, and test authoring by VI users.
